# Taxonomy of Relational Warming Methodologies

### A Systematic Analysis of Prompts, Guidances, and Experiential Mechanics in Authentic Relating, Circling, and Restorative T-Groups

Relational warming methodologies represent a specialized suite of experiential exercises designed to safely transition groups from polite, superficial social norms into deep, present-moment interpersonal connection. Derived from the fields of Authentic Relating (AR), Circling, Gestalt therapy, and T-group dynamics, these exercises act as psychological disruptors to the automatic "intent to control" that governs approximately 90% of daily human interactions. Rather than engaging in transactional exchange or superficial "ice-breakers," relational warm-ups cultivate intersubjective mindfulness, somatic awareness, and mutual vulnerability.

This report provides an exhaustive, systematically categorised compilation of authentic warming exercises, prompts, and facilitator guidances extracted from established relational materials and session formats.

---

## Theoretical Foundations of Interpersonal Warming

The transition from conventional social discourse to relational intimacy is governed by clear psychological and somatic principles. Under normal conditions, individuals operate within what situational awareness models define as "Condition White" (a state of distraction or relational unawareness) or a highly guarded "Condition Yellow" (relaxed alertness to environmental cues). Standard social scripts serve as protective barriers to shield individuals from the discomfort of being truly seen.

To dismantle these protective layers without triggering psychological panic, relational facilitators employ structured containers. This process is grounded in the **five core practices of Authentic Relating**, originally compiled by ART International and expanded across global communities:

1. **Welcome Everything:** Fostering an experiential field where all arising emotions, somatic sensations, and resistances are acknowledged rather than suppressed.
2. **Assume Nothing:** Actively noticing mental projections, stories, and cognitive interpretations, and systematically checking them against immediate reality.
3. **Reveal Your Experience:** Sharing real-time internal data, thereby exposing one's raw humanity to the group.
4. **Own Your Experience:** Formulating communication from a position of absolute self-responsibility, utilising "I" statements to reflect internal states rather than blaming external triggers.
5. **Honor Self and Other:** Balancing individual boundaries and needs with a deep respect for the psychological reality of the other person.

Warming exercises manipulate these social rules. By introducing artificial constraints—such as timed responses, restricted dialogue stems, or non-verbal prompts—the facilitator bypasses the intellectualised ego, allowing the group to enter a state of shared emotional synchrony and relational flow.

These dyadic structures focus entirely on the real-time intersubjective field, training participants in active feedback, raw vulnerability, and present-moment awareness.

## Susan Campbell's Ten Truth Skills

In *Saying What's Real*, Susan Campbell outlines ten fundamental "Truth Skills" designed to replace unconscious control patterns with honest, spontaneous self-expression. These skills function as relational warm-ups, helping participants become aware of their defensive habits and ground themselves in authentic communication.

| Truth Skill | Psychodynamics & Behavioral Mechanism | Clinical/Experiential Prompting Script | References |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **1. Experiencing What Is** | Developing a felt, body-based awareness of present sensations, while separating raw experience from mental judgements and projections. | "Pause and direct your attention inward. What somatic sensations are present in your chest, belly, or throat? Can you notice your thoughts without identifying with them?" | susancampbell.com [cite: 32, 33] |
| **2. Being Transparent** | Disclosing current feelings, sensations, or mental narratives with the simple goal of "knowing and being known," free from the need to defend or explain. | "Disclose your immediate experience to your partner. Share exactly what you are feeling or imagining right now, without explaining why or defending your position." | susancampbell.com [cite: 32, 33] |
| **3. Knowing Your Intent** | Reflecting on whether your communication is intended to relate (reveal yourself) or to control (manage impressions, avoid conflict, or manipulate outcomes). | "Before you speak, ask yourself: Is my goal to reveal my true self, or am I trying to manage how they perceive me or control how they react?" | susancampbell.com [cite: 4, 32, 33] |
| **4. Asserting What You Want & Don't Want** | Expressing desires and boundaries clearly, without expecting to always get your way, while remaining sensitive to others' limits. | "Clearly state one desire or boundary to your partner. Use the formula: 'I want...' or 'I don't want...', without adding explanations or justifications." | susancampbell.com [cite: 32, 33] |
| **5. Thriving on Feedback** | Cultivating active curiosity about how others perceive and react to you, without becoming dependent on their validation. | "Ask your partner: 'What is your immediate reaction or impression of me right now?' Listen to their response with open curiosity." | susancampbell.com [cite: 32, 33] |
| **6. Taking Back Projections** | Recognising when you are triggered, and separating your mental "fear-stories" from what actually occurred. | "Reflect on a recent moment where your buttons were pushed. Ask: What actually happened, and what is the fear-story I created around it?" | susancampbell.com [cite: 32, 33] |
| **7. Revising an Earlier Statement** | Revisiting a past interaction when your feelings change, or when you realise you spoke automatically or untruthfully. | "If you could repeat that interaction, what would you say differently? Use the phrase: 'If I had it to do over, what is truer for me now is...'" | susancampbell.com [cite: 32, 33] |
| **8. Holding Differences** | Empathising with another's viewpoint while holding a different view, allowing yourself to sit with that relational tension. | "Can you feel your own perspective while simultaneously allowing your partner's different perspective to exist in your awareness? Avoid the urge to debate." | susancampbell.com [cite: 32, 33] |
| **9. Sharing Mixed Emotions** | Communicating multiple, conflicting feelings about a situation (such as wanting to be honest while fearing you might hurt the other person). | "Disclose your conflicting feelings. For example: 'I want to clear the air with you, but I'm also feeling afraid that my words might hurt you.'" | susancampbell.com [cite: 32, 33] |
| **10. Embracing Silence** | Allowing non-verbal, spacious pauses in conversation without rushing to fill them with explanations, excuses, or small talk. | "After your partner speaks, pause. Take one full breath before responding. Allow the silence to exist, and notice the non-verbal energy between you." | susancampbell.com [cite: 32, 33] |

## Operational Mechanics and Facilitation Design

The success of relational warming relies on precise timing, clear boundaries, and structured session design. Facilitators must manage the emotional energy of the room to ensure participants are engaged but not overwhelmed.

### 1. The Standard Game Leader Format (Two-Hour Meeting)

For a group of 6 to 10 participants, a two-hour session should follow a structured sequence to build connection and psychological safety.

```
[0:00 - 0:15]  Introductions & Context-Setting
      │
[0:15 - 0:35]  Pair Warm-Up Games (Best Friends, Noticing Game)
      │
[0:35 - 1:45]  Main Game (The Hot Seat & Appreciation)
      │
[1:45 - 2:00]  Checkout & Integration
```

- **Introductions (15 Minutes):** Each participant shares their name, why they are attending, and something unique about themselves.
- **Context-Setting:** The leader establishes the group agreements and shares a brief history of the practice (origins in California in 1999 as Circling, later expanding into games). The leader then conducts a brief, "popcorn-style" reflection round, asking: "What lands for you, and what makes you feel resistant?".
- **Pair Warm-Up Games (20 Minutes):** Arrange participants in pairs. Instruct them to play one round of the Best Friends Game (3 minutes per partner), followed by one or two rounds of The Noticing Game (4 to 5 minutes total).
- **The Hot Seat & Appreciation Round (70 Minutes):** In a group of 8, each participant receives a 7-minute hot seat followed by a 3-minute appreciation round. The person on the hot seat receives the group's undivided attention and answers questions according to their chosen intensity level. The session leader must keep time strictly, holding up fingers to show the remaining minutes (3, 2, 1, 0) to help participants relax into a safe structure.
- **Checkout Round (15 Minutes):** The session concludes with a round where everyone shares how they are feeling somatic-style and what they are taking home.

### 2. Physical and Virtual Space Considerations

In physical settings, facilitators should set up chairs in a clear circle, keep the space tidy, and provide light snacks during breaks. Participants are often asked to remove their shoes to encourage a relaxed, grounded presence.

For virtual settings (such as Zoom), facilitators can adapt exercises using specific platform features:

- **Anybody Else (Zoom Variation):** Participants use Zoom's "Hide non-video participants" feature. Everyone turns their camera off to start. One participant shares a statement: "Anybody else has felt completely alone in a crowded room?". Everyone for whom this is true turns their video on, providing a striking visual map of shared human experience.
- **The Chat Feature:** Facilitators can post clear prompts, timing updates, and instructions directly in the chat so partners can refer back to them during breakout rooms.

### 3. Alternative Three-Hour Format

For a longer session, facilitators can extend the meeting to three hours by adding a 20 to 30 minute socializing break at the halfway mark. This pause allows participants to step away from intense focus, integrate their experiences, and connect casually with others.

## Psychodynamics and Second-Order Relational Effects

Relational warming exercises go beyond simple icebreakers; they can trigger complex psychodynamic shifts as participants let go of their typical social defenses.

```
               [Intimate Relational Container]
                              │
         ┌────────────────────┴────────────────────┐
         ▼                                          ▼
   [Transference]                             [Projection]
 - Group projects past figures              - Unconscious discomfort assigned
   or family roles onto others.               to another participant.
```

### 1. Transference, Projection, and Unconscious Defenses

As participants step out of normal social scripts, they encounter a highly ambiguous, emotionally open space. This setting often triggers unconscious psychological defenses:

- **Projection in Action:** When a participant cannot tolerate their own relational anxiety or judgments, they project them onto others. For example, during The Noticing Game, a participant might say, "I notice you seem intensely angry with me," when they are actually struggling with their own unexpressed anger or fear of rejection.
- **Transference Dynamics:** The unstructured, intimate nature of Circling and T-groups often causes participants to project past family dynamics or authority figures onto the facilitator or other group members. A participant might react defensively to the facilitator's timing boundaries, projecting an old authority dynamic onto the leader.
- **Denial and Repression:** Participants may unconsciously deny their pain or discomfort to appear composed. Exercises like Susan Campbell's Need State Check-In help participants uncover these repressed states, transforming somatic tension back into conscious awareness.

### 2. The "Opposite Impact" Phenomenon

In deep relational fields, verbal expressions often produce counter-intuitive psychological reactions. For example, a polite, standard compliment can sometimes make the recipient feel isolated or patronized, as it may feel like an attempt to play it safe. Conversely, a raw, honest expression of irritation or disappointment can serve as the initial seed of deep trust and safety, proving that the relationship can withstand real, unfiltered truths.

### 3. The Distinction Between Circling and Authentic Relating

While closely related, Circling and Authentic Relating operate on different assumptions and methods:

- **Formal Circling:** Relies on the explicit agreement to "welcome everything" within a structured group container. It requires a formal, non-reactive space where participants can process whatever arises in the present moment. It is a specialized practice that cannot be assumed to work in daily life, where people may naturally respond to strong emotions with judgment or defensiveness.
- **Authentic Relating:** Operates on the broader human assumption that everyone desires to love, be loved, and belong. It can be practiced in daily life without prior agreements. It focuses on leading with vulnerability and offering quality, compassionate attention to counteract isolation and build trust in everyday interactions.

## Professional Recommendations for Facilitators

Based on compiled session data and relational manuals, facilitators should implement the following recommendations to maximize connection and maintain psychological safety:

1. **Model Vulnerability First:** Never lead an exercise you have not personally experienced and dropped into deeply. Participants will only match the depth of vulnerability that the facilitator is willing to model.
2. **Focus on Witnessing and Affirming:** As a general rule of thumb, 80% of a facilitator's job is to witness and affirm, 15% is to show vulnerability, and only 5% is to challenge or redirect. When feeling stuck, focus entirely on witnessing.
3. **Intervene and Redirect Gently:** Do not hesitate to step in when a participant's share turns into coaching, advice-giving, or analyzing. Ask permission first: "Can I challenge you on something? That felt more like advice than personal impact. Would you be willing to try that again from a feeling place?".
4. **Maintain Strict Timing Boundaries:** Keep timed exercises precise. Participants feel safer and can relax more easily when they trust that the facilitator will maintain fair, clear structures. Use a 30-second warning before calling time to help pairs wrap up their connection smoothly.
5. **Welcome All Responses, Especially Upsets:** If a participant becomes upset or resistant, do not try to fix or change their state. Welcome the reaction fully, asking: "What is happening for you right now? Can we be with exactly what is coming up?". This approach helps transform tension into a moment of deep connection.
